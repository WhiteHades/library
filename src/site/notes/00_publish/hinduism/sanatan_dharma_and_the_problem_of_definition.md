# Sanatan dharma and the problem of definition

## quick read

- Before reaching caste or philosophy, the question of definition demands attention.
- `Sanatan dharma` is not held still for long. It widens, narrows, and shifts depending on who is defining it.
- A truth claim that changes scope during examination becomes harder to test and harder to teach.

The definitional problem arrives almost immediately. Some traditions widen `sanatan dharma` to include Buddhism, Jainism, and Sikhism alongside Hinduism. That answer is clever, but it also changes the subject. If the original question is about Hinduism, widening the category may ease pressure, yet it also makes the centre less clear.

This instability shows up elsewhere too. One moment the issue is Hinduism in general. The next moment it is Vaishnavism. Then it becomes ISKCON. Then it becomes Advaita. Then it becomes the Gita as the highest lens. Then a different authority is rejected because he belongs to the wrong school. At several points, the difficulty is not that Hinduism has variety. Many religions have variety. The difficulty is that the boundary between legitimate variety and plain contradiction becomes difficult to hold.

The problem is sharpened by the absence of a single prophet, a single creed, or a single public criterion of entry and exit. Regional gods, sectarian supremacy, different readings of Vishnu, Krishna, Shiva, and the status of devas all feed into this. Even the eastern spread question sharpens it: if some communities keep the name Hindu while dropping markers that other Hindus treat as central, what exactly remained constant?

The result is a basic but serious pressure point. Before asking whether Hinduism is universally true, one has to ask what stable object is being tested. If the answer changes from school to school and from argument to argument, the claim to universality weakens before the larger question even begins.
