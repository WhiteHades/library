# Mission and the problem of Hindu universality

## quick read

- One of the cleanest questions in the material is this: if Hinduism is true for everyone, why was so little of it clearly offered to everyone?
- The discussions do not deny Hindu influence abroad. They distinguish between export of practice and spread of creed.
- Yoga, meditation, breathwork, and cultural atmosphere travel much more easily in the material than a stable doctrinal invitation does.

## note

A religion can stay local simply because it does not push itself outward with enough clarity. That is one of the most persistent claims running through these discussions. Some Hindu callers say Hinduism is not missionary by nature. Some imply that it does not need to convert outsiders. Later, the Hare Krishna guest insists that his Vaishnava line is explicitly missionary and global. That answer matters, but it only partially relieves the problem. It shows that missionary Hinduism exists. It also shows that the missionary impulse is not straightforwardly pan-Hindu.

That difference is important. A sect can travel strongly while the wider religious umbrella remains unstable in definition and weak in common mission. This is why the material keeps pressing beyond the existence of ISKCON. The question is not whether some Hindus preach. The question is whether Hinduism, taken as a universal truth claim, has historically moved with the force and clarity that such a claim would normally produce.

The discussions treat yoga as the clearest example of partial export. Yoga travels. Meditation travels. Breathwork travels. But these often travel as techniques, therapies, or softened spiritual ambience. In the debate, this becomes a serious point rather than a cheap one. A technique can move into offices, wellness culture, and self-help spaces without bringing with it the whole metaphysical or scriptural world from which it came.

That is why the phrase `Trojan horse` keeps hovering over this part of the material. The claim is not that every teacher is consciously deceptive. The claim is that Hindu influence often spreads through fragments first and creed second, if creed arrives at all. A universal truth, by contrast, should be able to name itself plainly.
