# Mission and the problem of Hindu universality

A religion can stay local simply because it does not try very hard to travel.

That is one of the cleanest pressure points in the stream. If Hinduism is true for everyone, why was so little of it clearly offered to everyone? Some callers say Hindus are not missionary by nature. Others say they do not need to invite outsiders. Others point to groups like ISKCON as partial exceptions. None of those replies really removes the tension.

The panel's broader point is plain. Yoga spread farther than Hinduism because techniques are easier to export than theology. Breathwork can enter an office. Aesthetic fragments can enter wellness culture. A civilisational religion tied to lineage, sect, and inherited practice does not move so easily. When it does move, it often arrives wrapped in calmness rather than conviction.

That is why the stream treats yoga as a Trojan horse. What gets exported is often the feeling first, and the doctrine later, if at all. The result is a religion that can influence the modern world without plainly converting it.

If a faith calls itself universal truth, that should produce some missionary impulse, however soft or gradual. When that impulse stays weak, unclear, or unnecessary, the claim to universality starts to sound like prestige without pressure.
