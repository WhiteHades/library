# Scripture, authority, and the problem of selective reading

## quick read

- The question is not only about what the texts say. It is also about who gets to decide which texts speak most loudly.
- Difficult passages are repeatedly absorbed by appeal to context, school, guru, mode, or changing age.
- That does not automatically make the readings false, but it does turn authority itself into part of the problem.

The question shifts from broad claims to stress tests. The stress tests are the awkward passages: pre-puberty marriage language in a Prabhupada purport, the Mohini-Shiva episode, Rama and Sita eating meat, and horse sacrifice with claims about the animal being elevated through ritual death. None of these are raised merely for shock. They test interpretive consistency.

The same pattern appears again and again. A troubling passage appears. The reply filters through one or more layers: this was for an earlier age, this applies only in a lower mode, this must be understood through guru lineage, this practice changed, this is not the highest teaching, or this school does not read the text that way. Each filter can be intelligible on its own. The cumulative effect, however, is that scriptural authority starts to feel unstable under pressure.

This is where the Gita and Upanishad hierarchy becomes important. At one point, the Gita is treated as decisive over apparently lower or earlier strands. At another point, it is still said to be the distilled milk of the Upanishadic cow. If one text depends on another for authority, yet can also overrule it in practice, then the hierarchy itself needs clearer explanation.

The real question, then, is not whether the texts contain difficult material. They plainly do. The question is whether the interpretive method handling that material remains stable enough to carry a universal truth claim.
