# Advaita and the problem of coherence

## quick read

- The question narrows from history to philosophy here.
- The central complaint is that Advaita's It is that its key terms seem to shift level whenever contradiction appears.
- A universal truth should become clearer under pressure, not harder to state plainly.

---

After caste and spread, the question narrows around a handful of words: Brahman, self, ego, karma, witness, reality. That is where the argument becomes exacting. The problem with Advaita is that it wants two things at once. It wants to say that Brahman alone is ultimately real, and yet it still needs karma, experience, perception, agency, and different states of awareness in order to talk about ordinary life.

The criticism is that Advaita survives scrutiny by changing levels of speech mid-stream. A thing exists, but only in a perceived sense. Karma functions, but not ultimately. The self is the witness, but the body acts. Different realities are invoked, then multiplicity is denied. The objection is not that layered language is always false, but that here the layering begins to look less like precision and more like retreat.

The scriptural background explains why this became so sharp. `Bhagavad Gita 3.27` and `14.19` are used to say that the gunas or prakriti perform action while the ego wrongly thinks `I am the doer`. The `two birds on one tree` passages from the `Muṇḍaka Upaniṣad` and `Śvetāśvatara Upaniṣad` distinguish the enjoying self from the witnessing Lord or higher Self. `Bhagavad Gita 16.8` is then brought in against the claim that the world is unreal and without God. These texts do not dissolve the problem. They explain why it became so knotted.

The problem, then, is not merely that Advaita is subtle. It is that its subtlety can become publicly evasive. A universal truth can be difficult, but it should not become impossible to pin down at exactly the moment it is asked to speak clearly.
